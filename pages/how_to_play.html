Spelldawn is not currently a game you can download and play. At time of writing, development is perhaps at most 20% complete, so everything here is extremely subject to change based on iteration and playtesting. The game doesn't currently have an in-game tutorial of any kind, so this serves as an introduction until we can make one.

If you're familiar with the rules of Android: Netrunner, the companion page Spelldawn for Netrunner Players might also prove helpful.

Introduction

Spelldawn is a card game for two players set in a high fantasy universe. One player acts as the Overlord, a dungeon master attempting to put their nefarious schemes into effect. The other player assumes the role of the Champion, a valiant hero attempting to delve into the dungeon and thwart the forces of evil.

Players in Spelldawn alternate taking turns, always beginning with the Overlord. During a player's turn, they have three action points () to spend on a variety of possible game effects such as gaining mana (), drawing cards, and playing cards. The Overlord can play and level up their scheme cards in the rooms of the dungeon in order to score points, while deploying minions to defend them. The Champion can initiate a raid on a room of the dungeon to attempt to bypass the dungeon's defenses and score points by foiling the Overlord's plans.

The objective of the game for both players is to score seven points. The Overlord scores points by leveling up their scheme cards, while the Champion scores points by initiating raids and stopping those schemes. The Champion also loses the game if they take too much damage (see Damage below), and both players lose the game if they must draw a card from an empty deck.

Game Overview

To start a game of Spelldawn, each player reveals their identity card to their opponent, a special card in play which can grant unique abilities throughout a game. Each player starts the game with 5 mana and draws 5 cards from their deck to form a starting hand. After drawing starting hands, the Overlord may choose to mulligan their hand, shuffling it back into their deck and drawing 5 new cards. After the Overlord has decided, the Champion decides whether to mulligan as well. The game then proceeds to the Overlord's first turn.

The play area in Spelldawn is broken up into eight distinct areas called Rooms. These represent the different places within the Overlord's dungeon in which they enact their plans.

Three of the rooms are for a special purpose, called inner rooms:

  • The Sanctum is a room which gives access to a card in the Overlord's hand
  • The Vault is a room which controls access to the top card of Overlord's deck
  • The Crypts give access to cards which the Overlord has discarded over the course of a game.

Other rooms are referred to as outer rooms.

Schools of Magic

In the world of Spelldawn, certain rare individuals can call upon powerful magical forces in order to accomplish their goals. Each person is primarily attuned to one of the three great schools of magic, allowing them to channel it into their spells for good or evil. Each card has a school associated with it and these are important for deckbuilding (see Deckbuilding below).

The schools of magic are:

  • Law: The magic of organization and order.
  • Primal: The magic of instinct and nature.
  • Shadow: The magic of deception and darkness.

In addition to the three schools, some cards have no school affiliation. These are called neutral cards and can be used in any deck.

Overlord Cards

There are five types of Overlord cards: identities, spells, schemes, minions, and projects. All cards except the identity card are shuffled into the Overlord deck at the start of the game. Overlord permanent cards are played face down into different rooms.

Overlord Identity

The identity card defines who the Overlord is in the universe of Spelldawn. It influences the Deckbuilding rules for the Overlord player, controlling the school to which cards in the deck must belong and how many cards are allowed from competing schools.

Identity cards start the game in play, and provide abilities which are active throughout the game.

Schemes

Scheme cards represent the Overlord's evil machinations to bring about their master plan. They are the only cards in Spelldawn that are worth scheme points.

The Overlord plays scheme cards face-down in outer rooms. Each scheme has a level requirement that must be met before the Overlord can score that card (see the Level Up a Room action). There can only ever be one scheme or one project in a room at a given time. Schemes have a Score ability which resolves when the Overlord meets the level requirement.

Minions are cards that the Overlord plays in order to defend the rooms of the dungeon against the Champion. Minions are played face down in rooms without paying their mana cost. They are automatically summoned and turned face up during a raid by paying their mana cost, if the Overlord has sufficient mana available.

Each minion has a Combat ability which fires if the Champion is unable to defeat this minion in combat during a raid, as well as a health and optionally a shield value. See the Encounter Phase section below for more information.

Projects

Projects provide the Overlord with resources in order to further their objectives. They're played in outer rooms in the same manner as schemes without paying their mana cost. Projects can specify an Unveil condition which causes them to be turned face up if the Overlord has sufficient mana available to pay their cost.

Some projects with the Level Up keyword can be leveled up in the same manner as schemes, in order to mislead the Champion. When the Champion accesses a project they can destroy it by paying mana equal to its mana cost.

Champion Cards

There are four types of Champion cards: identities, weapons, artifacts, and spells. As with the Overlord, the non-identity cards are shuffled together to create the Champion's deck. Champion cards are always played face-up.

Champion Identity

As with the Overlord, the Champion's identity card defines the character the Champion is playing as in the current game. The same Deckbuilding restrictions apply to the Champion deck. The Champion identity card starts the game in play and provides ongoing abilities throughout the game.

Weapons

Weapons are used by the Champion to defeat the Overlord's minons. The Champion plays weapons by paying their mana cost. During a raid, a weapon can be used to attack and defeat a minion if it is of the correct Lineage and its attack value equals or exceeds the minion's health.

Each weapon starts with a base attack value which is shown on the card. Many weapons also have abilities which can increase their attack value during combat by paying mana.

Turn Sequence

Players in Spelldawn alternate taking turns, starting with the Overlord player. Players are never required to take an action during their opponenet's turn. Each player begins their turn with (3 action points). A turn consists of these phases:

  1. Start of Turn Phase
  2. Draw Phase
  3. Main Phase
  4. Discard Phase

Start of Turn Phase

During the Start of Turn phase, triggered start-of-turn abilities on cards in play fire. The start of the Champion's turn is called Dawn and any cards with Dawn trigger their abilities in this phase. The start of the Overlord's turn is called Dusk and fires Dusk abilities.

Draw Phase

The active player draws one card from their deck. If their deck is empty, that player immediately loses the game.

Main Phase

The player takes actions by spending . All action points must be spent during the main phase, after which the discard phase immediately begins.

The active player can take the following actions as many times as they like:

  • Gain 1 (one mana)
  • Draw a card
  • Play a card from hand
  • , 1 Level up a room (Overlord)
  • Initiate a raid (Champion)
  • Activate an ability of a card in play

Gain Mana

The player spends to increase their mana pool by 1

Draw a Card

For , the player draws one card from their deck. This action cannot be taken if the deck is currently empty.

Play a Card

The player pays along with any required costs for a card in hand in order to play it.

  • Spell cards require their mana cost to be paid immediately and resolve their effects immediately. They are then moved to the discard pile.
  • Artifact and Weapon cards require their mana cost to be paid immediately and are put into play face up on the Champion's side of the game arena.
  • Scheme cards are played face down in an empty outer room that doesn't currently contain a scheme or project. They do not have mana costs.
  • Project cards do not require their mana cost to be paid immediately. They are played face down in an outer room that doesn't currently contain a scheme or project.
  • Minion cards do not require their mana cost to be paid immediately. They are put into play face down as the outermost defender in a room. A maximum of 4 minions can be in a room at any time. If an additional minion is played, the innermost minion is immediately moved to the Crypts.

Level Up a Room

By paying and 1, the Overlord can pick an outer room to level up. They place one level counter on each card present in that room which can be leveled up. Cards other than schemes can only be leveled up if they have the Level Up keyword. The level up action cannot be taken on a room that has no cards that can be leveled up.

When the number of level counters on a scheme card is greater than or equal to its level requirement, that card will immediately be scored. The scheme card is turned face up, and then any Score abilities trigger. The Overlord gains scheme points equal to the points value of the card and it moves to their score area.

Initiate a Raid

For , the Champion can initiate a raid against one of the Overlord's non-empty rooms in order to score schemes and destroy projects. See Raids for more information.

Activate an Abilitiy

Some cards have abilities listed on them. These are called activated abilities and can only be used during the owning player's Main phase by paying all of the listed costs.

Discard Phase

Finally, the active player automatically discards down to their maximum hand size. Players begin the game with a maximum hand size of 7 cards, but card effects can increase or decrease this limit. If more cards are present in hand than their maximum hand size, players must discard their leftmost card until they are no longer above the maximum.

Lineage Types

The world of Spelldawn is undergoing a confluence between three planes of existence. The original terrestrial plane is being overlaid by two other planes, the infernal plane and the abyssal plane, and creatures and items from those other worlds are now passing through into the mortal plane.

Thus, there are three main types of weapons and minions in Spelldawn, called the lineage of that card. They are:

  • Mortal: Weapons and creatures from the original prime material plane.
  • Infernal: Weapons and creatures from Infernum, a kingdom of flame and soot which exists in a perpetual state of war.
  • Abyssal: Weapons and creatures from The Abyss, the realm of darkness ruled over by the tyrannical ancient Demonlords.

Because of the nature of planar magical resonance, minions can only be damaged by a weapon from a matching lineage -- anyone attempt to wield a mortal weapon against a creature of Infernum will soon discover that their blade simply passes through the enemy like slicing through air!

In addition to the main lineage types, some weapons and minions have special properties.

  • Prismatic weapons are rare and powerful magical artifacts which have the ability to damage enemies from any lineage.
  • Construct minions are mechanical assemblages which have disadvantage of being able to be damaged by any weapon type.

Raids

During a raid, the Champion attacks one of the Overlord's rooms in order to foil their schemes and destroy ongoing projects. They encounter each minion defending the room in sequence, either defeating that minion using their weapons or suffering its combat effects.

Raids are broken up into three phases:

  1. Start of Raid Phase
  2. Encounter Phase
  3. Access Phase

Starting a Raid

When a raid is declared on a room, any Raid abilities on cards in play trigger. Next, the game checks if the room is defended by any minions which are face-up or which the Overlord has sufficient mana available to summon. If any such minion exists, the raid proceeds to the Encounter phase, otherwise it moves to the Access phase.

Encounter Phase

Approaching Minions

During the encounter phase, the Champion approaches each minion in the target room in sequence, starting with the outermost minion. If the defending minion is face-down and the Overlord currently has mana available to pay its listed summon cost, the minion is summoned, turning it face up. Any Summon abilities on the card trigger.

If the approached minion is face-up, the Champion encounters that minion. Otherwise, the Champion approaches the next minion in sequence, or proceeds to the Access phase if no minions remain.

Encountering a Minion

When a minion is encountered, the Champion must either defeat the minion or continue and have its Combat ability fire.

In order to defeat a minion, the Champion must do two things:

  • Pay an amount of mana equal to the shield value of the minion, if any. Some card effects such as the Breach keyword reduce the amount that must be paid for shield values.
  • Deal damage to the minion equal to its health value. Weapons with the same lineage type as the minion deal damage equal to their base attack value plus any bonuses from abilities.

Defeat is always temporary for the Overlord's minions, as they are soulbound to their post by the summoning ritual and resurrect after a few hours. The minion is not removed from the room when defeated, but stays in play -- ready for the next raid.

Once the minion is either defeated or has used its combat abilities, the next minion in sequence is approached, or the raid moves to the Access phase.

Attack Boosts

Many weapons have printed abilities which temporarily increase their attack value, called an attack boost, in the form of a cost to add some amount of attack. Unless otherwise indicated, this cost can be paid any number of times to reach the target, and the increased attack value lasts only for the duration of the current minion encounter.

For example, if the Champion has a weapon with a base attack of 3 and a boost ability "2: +1 Attack", the champion can pay 2 per attack point required. If they are attempting to defeat a minion with 5 health, they can do so by paying 4 to activate the attack boost twice. The weapon's attack value then reverts to 3.

Access Phase

Raid Success

Once all of the minions defending the target room have been passed, the raid is considered a success, and any Successful Raid triggers on cards in play fire. The Champion then accesses cards as follows based on the target room:

  • Sanctum: The Champion accesses one random card from the Overlord's hand.
  • Vault: The Champion accesses the top card of the Overlord's deck. The accessed card is not revealed to the Overlord.
  • Crypts: The Champion accesses all face-down cards in the Overlord's discard pile.
  • Outer Room: The Champion accesses the project or server card occupying the room.

Accessing Cards

When the Champion accesses cards, the actions they can take vary based on the type of the accessed card:

  • If the Champion acceses a scheme card they may score it, gaining scheme points equal to its points value and then moving it to their score area. This does not trigger the Score ability of the card.
  • If the Champion accesses a project card, it becomes revealed. The Champion may destroy it by paying its unveil cost and then moving it to the Overlord's discard pile.
  • Other card types have no effect when accessed other than becoming revealed to the Champion. The revealed state of cards persists across game zones, for example they continue to be revealed when a minion is played from hand into a specific room.

When accessing multiple cards, the Champion may take these actions in any order. After concluding access actions, the raid ends and the Champion's main phase resumes.

Other Card Abilities

Damage

Some cards and abilities deal damage to the Champion. When the Champion takes damage, they must discard one card from their hand at random per point of damage dealt. If the Champion takes more damage than the number of cards in their hand, they immediately lose the game.

Curses

A card ability may apply a curse to the Champion. Curses are spell cards with the text "Cannot be discarded" which are generated in the Champion's hand. They may also have other ongoing effects while in hand. Curses have a mana cost of 2. While the Champion has a curse card in hand they are said to be cursed.

A curse card can never be discarded to damage or to hand size during the discard phase. However, curses can be removed from hand by playing them normally via the 'Play Card' action, spending and 2. If the Champion's hand consists only of curse cards when they take damage, they lose the game due to being unable to discard a card.

Winning the Game

There are three primary ways in which a game of Spelldawn can end:

  • If at any time a player has 7 or more points, they immediately win the game
  • If a player attempts to draws a card from an empty deck, their opponenent wins the game.
  • If the Champion takes damage and is unable to discard a card, the Overlord wins the game.

Deckbuilding

A deck in Spelldawn consists of 50 regular cards and an identity card. Decks are associated with a specific side (Overlord or Champion) and a primary school of magic. Decks cannot contain more than 3 copies of any card by name.

The chosen identity card must match the school for the deck. Decks can contain any number of neutral cards and cards from that school. In addition, the identity card gives a number of competing school cards allowed in the deck. For example, a Shadow deck whose identity displays the number 6 may contain any number of Shadow and neutral cards, but can contain at most 6 Law or Primal cards.

The Overlord deck must contain at least 20 scheme points worth of scheme cards.

Rarity and Game Rewards

The winner of a game of Spelldawn receives a pack of 5 cards as a reward. Cards are available in four levels of rarity: Common, Rare, Exalted, and Legendary.

The first four cards in a pack occupy normal slots and can contain any rarity of cards, in this distribution:

  • Common: 80%
  • Rare: 15%
  • Exalted: 4%
  • Legendary: 1%

The fifth card in a pack occupies the rare slot and always contains a rare card or better, following this distribution:

  • Rare: 75%
  • Exalted: 20%
  • Legendary: 5%

Card packs are conceptually generated by drawing without replacement from a player's reward deck, a collection which initially contains 3 copies of every card in the game. This means that packs are more likely to contain cards that have not previously been collected, and can never contain cards that a player already has 3 copies of. If the reward deck is empty for a given rarity level, pack generation reverts to random selection.

Experience Points

The loser of a game of Spelldawn does not receive any cards, but they do gain the benefit of experience, learning from their defeat in order to improve in the future. This player earns 60 experience points after losing a game. 100 experience points can be redeemed for a card pack.
